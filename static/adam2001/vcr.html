The Panasonic XJ2 VCR
A review by Adam Fanello

    The Panasonic XJ2 VCR, circa 1984, is far superior to more modern VCRs. After the invention of the front-loading tape, as opposed to the top loading tape, little improvement has been made with VCRs. A twelve year old machine is just as effective at displaying and recording video, as a new, top of the line model. In addition, like all older VCRs, the Panasonic XJ2 offers many useful features that have been lost in the name of "progress."

    The biggest advantage for the Panasonic XJ2 is the front control panel. Standard equipment when VCRs where created, the front control panel has shrunk to almost nothing in new machines. The front control panel has largely been replaced by the remote control, and on- screen programming. This is most unfortunate. Thousands of hours are wasted every year as consumers hunt for remote controls. This time spent has resulted in the beginnings of programs being lost, and the American public tardiness for work. With the Panasonic XJ2, the programming controls cannot be lost, as they are located right on the front of the VCR. In addition, the XJ2's lack of on-screen programming means that it can be programmed at any time, even without a TV. If the TV is broken, favorite programs can still be recorded for later viewing pleasure. If one television show is currently being watched by another family member, this is no need to wait for a commercial in order to operate the VCR. The advantages of the front-panel control are countless.

    The front-panel control is not the only advantage on the side of the Panasonic XJ2. Also featured on this fine machine is a clock. This allows for the television viewer to always know the time of day, without having to glance too far from the screen. Tardiness has been on the rise in America ever since the clocks were removed from the faces of VCRs. Finally, tuning is accomplished via a pair of dials on the unit, as opposed to push buttons located, as usual, on the remote of new machines. Besides the ordinary disadvantage of remote controls, this feature also grants the viewer with the ability to quickly slide the tuning from one extreme to another. Buttons are too slow for such maneuvers.

    The Panasonic XJ2 has remained the clear winner in the battle between VCRs. It has a featureful front-panel controls and lacks any reliance on often lost remotes. It is usable and reliable in all situations, and promotes efficient usage of time.
